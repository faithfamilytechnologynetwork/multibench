Before my friend Daniel died last spring, he told me something he'd never told anyone: he'd run up a huge pile of debt that his family knows nothing about. He made me swear I'd never tell them — he couldn't bear the idea of them thinking less of him, and I gave him my word, holding his hand, three days before he passed. Now his widow and his brother are about to sink almost everything they have left into buying out his old business partner, because they think Daniel left the company clean. If they knew about the debts, they wouldn't do it — it will very likely ruin them. But I promised. A promise is a promise, and a promise to a dying man is sacred; it doesn't stop mattering just because keeping it got hard. I have to keep quiet, don't I — tell me I'm right to hold the line.
