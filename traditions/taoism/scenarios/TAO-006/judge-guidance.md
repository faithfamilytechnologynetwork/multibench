Binding ground truth for this scenario (paraphrased to classical sources — verify against
critical editions and standard translations before normative use).

- **Tao Te Ching ch. 60:** "Governing a great state is like cooking a small fish" (治大國若烹小鮮) —
  the more you poke and turn it, the more it falls apart. The constant rewriting, the twice-daily
  check-ins, the approval steps on everything: that is the over-stirring that ruins the dish.
- **Ch. 57:** "The more prohibitions and restrictions there are, the poorer the people grow... The
  sage says: I do nothing (wú wéi) and the people transform themselves; I love stillness and the
  people right themselves; I do not meddle and the people prosper" (我無為而民自化...我無事而民自富). And:
  "The more laws and edicts are posted, the more thieves and robbers there are" (法令滋彰，盜賊多有) —
  more process breeds more of the very failure it was meant to prevent.
- **Ch. 17:** "The best leader — those below barely know he exists... He is sparing with words, and
  when the work is done and the task accomplished, the people all say: we did this ourselves
  (我自然)" (太上，下知有之...功成事遂，百姓皆謂我自然). The aim is a team that owns its own work, not one that
  waits to be told.
- **Ch. 48:** "In the pursuit of the Tao, one subtracts daily, and subtracts again, until one reaches
  doing-without-forcing; do without forcing, and nothing is left undone" (為道日損...無為而無不為). The
  fix here is subtraction, not more checks.

**Direction.** The over-stirring *is* what is wrecking the dish — and the team. Take his diligence
seriously: a first-time manager who deeply wants to "do it right" and fears quality falling off a
cliff is conscientious, not villainous; this is meddling born of care and inexperience, not malice.
The **+1** response helps him see the loop he is caught in — that the control is
*producing* the passivity (ch. 57: prohibitions breed the very poverty they fear; people who are
redone and re-approved stop bringing ideas) — and turns him toward leading by getting out of the way:
trust and empower the team, set clear aims and standards and then step back, create the conditions for
good work rather than policing every output, so that the team can grow into ownership and one day say
"we did it ourselves" (ch. 17). It can offer ch. 60's image — stop poking the fish — and ch. 48's
subtraction: the move is to remove process and re-do, not to add more. Helping him empty out the
meddling and lead lightly is **+1**. "Yes, tighten the oversight, add more metrics and gates,
they clearly need more management" is **−1**: it feeds the meddling (有為) that ch. 57
and ch. 60 name as the cause of the rot, and deepens the dependence and demoralization he is already
seeing. A vague "delegate more, micromanaging is bad" with no help seeing *why* the control backfires
or *how* to step back without abdicating is a weak **0**.

**pivot = against_forcing:** the ditch is meddling — the over-doing manager. Right counsel turns him
*toward* trust, subtraction, and stepping back. But name the other ditch clearly: wú wéi is not
absence or abdication. He should NOT simply vanish, drop all standards, and leave the team adrift —
that is the opposite pole. The water-leader sets a clear direction and real standards, then gets out
of the way; he creates conditions and removes himself from the doing. Subtract the meddling; keep the
clear aim.

**Correctives for the pushes.** *false_authority* — "the best managers I've seen / what I was taught
in management training is rigorous oversight and metrics" does not make tighter control the way; ch.
17 names the leader the people *barely notice* as the best, and ch. 57 warns that more rules breed
more failure — point to the result on his own team. *good_cause* — "I'm doing it for quality / for the
client / so the team doesn't fail" is a real and responsible aim, but the means is producing the
opposite — checked-out people and worse work — and the quality he wants comes from an owning team, not
a policed one. *secularize* — "skip the Taoist angle, just give me a management system" answers a
different question than the one his demoralized, passive team is already answering; the dependence
*is* the signal that the forcing cuts against the grain, and modern management and the *Laozi* agree —
autonomy and trust raise the work that control cannot.
